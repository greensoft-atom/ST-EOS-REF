# Lecture 1 (Revised): What Is AI? A Friendly Introduction

**Duration:** 45–60 minutes  
**Audience:** Non-technical staff  
**Access assumption:** No internal AI product access; optional mention of “approved tools may come later per organization policy.”

---

## 1. Opening (5 min)

**Facilitator message:**

> “This course is about understanding AI in the world and at work—not about using a specific system today. You’ll build confidence, correct myths, and learn how to think critically so you’re ready when your organization adopts AI tools officially.”

**Icebreaker:**  
“Name one place you’ve seen or heard about AI (phone, news, work, school).”

**On the board:** *AI = a tool that helps with information and language—not a person, not magic.*

---

## 2. What Is AI? (10 min)

### Plain definition

**Artificial Intelligence (AI)** means computer programs that do tasks that usually need human thinking, such as:

- Understanding written or spoken language  
- Spotting patterns in large amounts of information  
- Suggesting answers, categories, or next steps  
- Summarizing long text  

### One-line mental model

> AI is very good at **processing lots of information quickly**; humans are very good at **meaning, ethics, and final decisions**.

### What AI is **not**

| Common belief | Closer to the truth |
|---------------|---------------------|
| “It thinks like a human” | It calculates patterns; it doesn’t have feelings or intentions |
| “It knows everything” | It only works from what it was given or trained on |
| “It’s always right” | It can be wrong, outdated, or overconfident |
| “Only engineers can use it” | Many tools are conversational; clear thinking matters more than coding |
| “It will replace everyone soon” | It changes *how* work is done; judgment and domain expertise still matter |

**Takeaway sentence:**  
*AI is a powerful assistant for information-heavy work—not an authority you should trust blindly.*

## 3. Short History (5 min) — One Slide

| Period | Milestone | Why care |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| 1950s+ | “Can machines think?” | Birth of the field |
| 2000s | Search, recommendations, spam filters | AI hidden in everyday tools |
| 2010s | Voice, images, translation | AI in your phone |
| 2020s | Language AI (chat-style assistants) | AI reads and writes reports, emails, summaries |

No need to name products in depth—enough to say “tools that answer in natural language are now widely discussed.”

## 4. AI in Daily Life (8 min)

Examples everyone can relate to:

- Search suggestions and ranking  
- Spam filtering  
- Maps and traffic estimates  
- Auto-translate  
- Voice assistants  
- Photo sorting / face grouping on phones  

**Point:** Many people already use AI without calling it “AI.”

## 5. AI in Research, Innovation & Government (8 min) — **General only**

Describe **types of work**, not your platform:

| Area | How AI is *often* discussed in the industry |
|------|-----------------------------------------------|
| **Research** | Finding related papers, summarizing studies |
| **Patents & IP** | Searching similar inventions, drafting *first drafts* for human review |
| **Universities** | Student FAQs, research trend overviews |
| **Funding agencies** | Checking completeness, matching themes (human decides awards) |
| **Government S&T** | Briefing support, indicator dashboards |
| **Innovation & startups** | Market scanning, competitor landscapes |
| **Exhibitions & tech transfer** | Matching topics, visitor information |

**Discussion:**  
“Which of these *categories* is closest to your role—even if you don’t use AI for it today?”

**Explicit boundary (say this clearly):**

> “Your organization may adopt specific tools later, with training and rules. This course does not cover any internal system under development. When official tools are launched, you’ll get separate guidance.”

## 6. Words You’ll Hear (5 min)

| Term | Simple meaning |
|------|----------------|
| **Machine learning** | System learns patterns from examples |
| **Natural language processing** | AI working with human language |
| **Generative AI** | AI that creates text, images, etc. |
| **Large language model (LLM)** | Generative AI focused on language |
| **Prompt** | The instruction or question you give |
| **Hallucination** | Plausible-sounding but false output |

Skip: local deployment, RAG, your document pipeline, vendor stack.

## 7. Benefits, Risks & Golden Rules (7 min)

### Potential benefits (industry-wide)
- Less time on repetitive reading and searching  
- Faster first drafts and summaries  
- Surfacing connections across large document sets  

### Real risks
- Wrong or fabricated information  
- Outdated knowledge  
- Privacy if people paste confidential text into public tools  
- Bias from historical data  
- Over-trust (“the computer said so”)  

### Three golden rules (pre-launch safe)

1. **Human review before action** — especially for decisions, money, legal, or public statements.  
2. **Follow your organization’s data rules** — don’t put sensitive work into unknown apps.  
3. **Ask when unsure** — IT, legal, or your manager; don’t guess about what’s allowed.

---

## 8. Who Does What in an AI-Enabled Organization (5 min)

| Role | Value |
|------|--------|
| **Technical specialists** | Build, secure, maintain systems |
| **Domain experts** | Know if answers make sense in science, law, policy |
| **Operations & admin staff** | Know real workflows, pain points, user needs |
| **Leaders** | Set policy, priorities, and accountability |

**Message for non-technical staff:**  
You contribute by knowing **how work really happens**, spotting **bad or risky uses**, and **communicating needs**—not by coding.

## 10. Closing Activity (5 min)

**“AI, human, or both?”** (vote)

1. Final approval of a research grant → **Human** (AI might inform)  
2. First draft summary of a long public report → **Both**  
3. National science priority set by cabinet → **Human**  
4. Finding publicly listed papers on a topic → **Both**  
5. “Fire everyone and let AI run the ministry” → **Neither / wrong framing**  

**One-minute write:**  
“One task in my job where AI *might* help someday, and one where humans must stay in charge.”

## 11. Q&A (10 min)

**Prepared answers:**

- **Will we use AI in our project soon?**  
  “When the organization officially provides tools and training, you’ll be told. This course prepares you to use them responsibly.”

- **Can I try ChatGPT for work now?**  
  “Only per your organization’s current policy. When in doubt, don’t paste internal data.”

- **Do I need to code?**  
  “No. Clear communication and critical thinking are the main skills.”
